Company Brain

One brain for the whole company. A private copy for every person.

Every employee gets a personal knowledge vault their tools keep current — from the command line to any AI assistant they connect. The company gets a shared memory that grows from everyone's work — without anyone's private notes ever leaking to anyone else.

The one idea to hold onto

There is a single master vault for the company. A piece of software called the compiler produces a filtered personal copy for each person. Anything you're not allowed to see is never written into your copy at all — so no app, no AI, and no mistake downstream can ever show it to you.

Why it exists

Every company brain before this one failed in one of two ways

Failure mode 1

The wiki that starves

The shared knowledge base nobody updates. Writing things down is extra work, so the wiki drifts out of date, people stop trusting it, and the company's real knowledge lives in inboxes and heads — and walks out the door with departures.

Failure mode 2

The sync that leaks

The tool that copies everything everywhere so it's never stale. Now your private notes — compensation conversations, candid feedback, half-formed ideas — are one search away from the whole company.

Company Brain is the middle path. Knowledge flows from personal notes into the shared brain continuously — but only through a step a human approves, and private material physically stays private. The shared brain neither starves nor leaks.

The striped bar is the whole point: other people's private material isn't hidden, locked, or filtered out of your copy — it was never put there.

The master vault

The single source of truth — one archive per company, holding every note as plain, readable files. Only the server ever touches it directly.

The compiler

The security wall. It rebuilds every personal copy each time the master changes, and it "fails closed": if anything goes wrong, the worst case is you briefly see less than you should — never more.

Write-back

When you edit your copy, the server checks every single change against what you're allowed to write before accepting it into the master. One out-of-bounds edit and the whole batch is declined.

The promotion queue — the human gate

The only way anything moves from a private area into a shared one. Your AI can draft a sanitized note and propose it; a person reviews and approves (or rejects) every single one.

Your AI assistant

Pointed at your vault alone. It files your meeting notes, tracks your action items, keeps your running memory current, and drafts what's worth sharing.

Search

Ask a question in plain language and get the right notes back — by keyword and by meaning. The index is built only from your own copy, so it inherits the same wall.

What lives inside

Where a note sits decides who sees it

There are no per-document permission checkboxes to get wrong. Every note lives in exactly one area, and the area itself is the permission.

The operation

A day in the life of one meeting

First: how does anything get in? Every brain has an Inbox, and capture is meant to feel like everyday habits — no new app to learn. There are five natural ways to send something to your brain.

✉

Email it

Your brain has its own address. Forward a thread, or fire off a note to yourself, and it arrives in your Inbox. Because it's tied to your email, the system knows it's you — a message from an address it doesn't recognize is turned away, never filed onto the wrong person.

💬

Message it

Text or DM your own brain like you'd message a colleague. Each person's chat assistant is locked to them alone, so a quick thought on the go lands in your Inbox and nowhere else.

🎙

Speak it

Record a voice memo after a call or between meetings. It's transcribed and dropped into your Inbox — the fastest way to capture something before it slips away.

📎

Drop, share, or type it

Save a transcript into your Inbox folder, or use your phone's "share" button to send a document straight to your brain. Or just hit Capture in your dashboard and type a thought — it names the exact Inbox it lands in before you save. If it's a file, it's ingestible — the original name is kept for reference.

🔌

Connect your tools

Services that take notes for you can deliver them automatically: a meeting recorder like Fathom, or automations built in Zapier, Make, or Composio. Each connection gets its own verified delivery door — every incoming delivery must prove it came from the service you authorized (a cryptographic signature or a shared secret), and repeats are recognized and filed only once.

Every one of these runs through a single, built-in intake step the company controls. Whichever channel you use — including outside services delivering on their own — the note passes through one safe entry point that confirms who's allowed to write where, checks each automated delivery's credentials, and stamps every note with where it came from. Capture stays effortless without loosening the privacy wall. (A shared, company-level assistant can also take in org-wide material like all-hands recordings.)

From there, everything is automatic. Here's the full journey of a single piece of information — say, the transcript of this morning's client call — from your Inbox to the company's shared memory.

  1. It lands in your Inbox You

    Via any of the routes above — emailed, messaged, spoken, dropped in, typed into the dashboard's Capture box, or delivered automatically by a connected tool. That's the entire effort required from you.

  2. Your AI files it Your AI

    Your assistant reads it, writes a summary, pulls out the decisions that were made and the action items (with owners and deadlines), and files each piece where it belongs. Anything it can't confidently place, it flags for you instead of guessing.

  3. Your edits sync back The server

    On a schedule, your changes flow back to the master vault — after the server verifies that every changed file is one you're allowed to write. Then everyone's personal copy is rebuilt fresh.

  4. Something looks worth sharing Your AI

    The call surfaced a pricing decision the whole company should know. Your AI drafts a sanitized version — only what's meant to be shared, with the source noted — and places it in your outgoing tray. It can propose; it can never publish. (You can propose one yourself, too, straight from a note in the dashboard.)

  5. A person approves it A human

    A designated owner reviews the pending queue — in the CLI or right in the admin dashboard's Promotions tab — and approves or rejects each draft after reading it. Approved notes land in the shared brain stamped with who wrote it, who approved it, and where it came from. Rejections keep a written reason — which teaches everyone what this company considers shareable.

  6. The whole company can find it Everyone

    On the next rebuild, the note appears in every person's copy. Six months from now, anyone can ask "why did we price it that way?" and get the decision — with its reasoning — in seconds.

The window in

A live view into what your brain knows

A brain you can't look inside is a brain you won't trust. So both sides of the system come with a dashboard — a live, local page that updates itself the moment the brain changes, served only to your own machine and reaching nothing but its own origin. (A single self-contained HTML snapshot is still one flag away when you want something to email or archive.)

For each person

Your dashboard

Open your vault in any browser and watch it stay current: how many notes you have and where they live, what's waiting in your inbox, your open action items, and an interactive map of your knowledge where every note is a dot connected to what it references — click one to see everything that links in and out.

Search by keyword and meaning, then read the note itself — rendered, with its backlinks beside it, in light or dark. And when a thought strikes, Capture writes it straight into your Inbox. Because it's built only from your copy, it can only ever show — and add to — what you're allowed to see.

Notes colored by area, sized by how connected they are.
For the operator

The company view

Whoever runs the system gets the whole picture at a glance: each person's vault — its size, how fresh its search index is, whether edits are waiting to sync back — the system's own health checks, and a table of exactly who may read and write each area.

It's also where the human gate lives: the Promotions tab shows each pending share with its destination, a warning naming who will be able to read it, and its full text — with Approve and Reject right there, and a badge that flags new arrivals from any tab. You approve under your own name, picked from the company's people list rather than typed, so the record can't carry a typo or a blank. What stays read-only is the permission configuration itself: it's edited in the audited files where every change leaves a record.

The dashboards observe by default and write only through the same guarded doors as everything else — capture can reach nothing but your own Inbox, approval still passes the human gate, and no view can rebuild an index or change a permission.

The trust story

Privacy is a guarantee, not a promise

Most AI tools handle privacy with instructions: the system tells the AI not to reveal things and hopes it listens. Company Brain doesn't ask. It's built so that the sensitive material isn't there to reveal.

A useful analogy

Think of a briefing binder prepared for each executive before a board meeting. Nobody hands everyone the master file and asks them not to peek at each other's sections — each person's binder is assembled to contain only their pages. Company Brain does that, automatically, for every person, on every update.

Absent, not hidden

Your copy physically contains only what you may read. There's no lock to pick and no filter to fool — what you can't see was never written to your copy. Even links pointing at notes outside your view are replaced with a stub, so the contents can't travel through them.

Failure only narrows, never widens

If the rebuild ever hits an error, your previous copy stays in place untouched. Every failure path leads to seeing less, temporarily — there is no error that shows you more.

The AI can propose, never publish

Your assistant has no ability to write into shared areas. The only door from private to shared is the promotion queue, and a human holds the key to it — every time, with a named approver on record.

Attacked on purpose, continuously

The privacy claim is tested adversarially: automated tests generate thousands of random company shapes — including planted "honeypot" content designed to escape — and verify that no one's private material ever survives into anyone else's copy.

The payoff

What you get, and what the company gets

For you

  • A memory that never lapses. Every meeting summarized, every action item tracked with its owner and deadline, every decision on record — without you doing the filing.
  • An assistant with full context. Your AI assistant reads from your always-current vault, so it never starts from zero and never confuses your work with someone else's.
  • Instant recall. Ask in plain language — "what did we agree with the client in March?" — and get the actual notes back, by keyword and by meaning.
  • A live map you can act on. Your dashboard shows what your brain holds and how it connects, updates as it changes, and lets you capture a new thought in a keystroke — so trust comes from looking, not believing.
  • Your private notes stay yours. Candid drafts and personal working notes are structurally invisible to everyone else, including their AIs.
  • Files you own. Everything is plain, readable documents on your machine — openable in Obsidian or any editor, with no lock-in.

For the company

  • Institutional memory that compounds. Decisions with their reasoning, playbooks, and client knowledge accumulate in one trustworthy place — and stay when people leave.
  • A shared brain that feeds itself. Because sharing is a one-click approval on an AI-prepared draft, the knowledge base actually gets updated — solving the failure that kills every wiki.
  • Faster onboarding and better decisions. New hires and decision-makers get grounded, searchable context — including the why behind past calls, not just the what.
  • Operations you can see. The company dashboard shows every vault's size, freshness, and pending activity, live — so the operator spots a stalled sync or a share awaiting approval the moment it happens.
  • Governance you can defend. Every shared note carries provenance: who wrote it, who approved it, where it came from. Every rejection carries a reason.
  • Start small, grow without rebuilding. A pilot runs with 5–25 people; scaling to 150+ means adding names to a roster, not re-architecting the system.
